About

Character-driven.
Built to perform.

I'm Beckham, a 19-year-old voice actor, content creator, and director. I specialize in animation, stop-motion, and video game voice work, with a natural midrange voice that goes deep and handles accents with ease.

Since early 2026, I've been deep in the work, booked roles across indie animated series, brickfilms, and a Steam game, with more always in the pipeline. I take direction well, deliver fast, and bring no ego to the booth.

Beyond voice acting, I'm building my own animated series (Mono No Aware), learning 2D and 3D animation, and working toward the long game: owning my own studio and creating original shows.

"Comfortable taking direction and delivering fast turnarounds. Not interested in audiobooks or comic dubbing, animation, games, and stop-motion are the focus."

The Concept

Bro and Dude are two aliens. They sit in lawn chairs in a Midwest backyard every night. They pass a joint. They talk about whatever is on their minds. There is no plot. There are no stakes. The location never changes. The format never changes.

What changes is what they talk about. Early episodes are low, casual, observational. From Episode 03 onward, the conversations start carrying real weight. Bro starts voicing opinions on things that matter. Dude pushes back. The comedy is always driving. What it's actually saying underneath is the show's business.

The Characters

BRO
The one with opinions
Look: Red beanie, red sweatshirt, jeans. Sitting forward, elbows on knees.
Energy: Grounded, assertive, quick to get heated. Always takes the first hit.
Role: The show's perspective. From Ep 03+, starts voicing real opinions. Needs to be checked.
Voice: Beckham Chadwick
DUDE
The one who keeps it together
Look: Blue beanie, blue sweatshirt, jeans. Slouched back in the chair.
Energy: Chill, loose, comfortable in silence. Slightly spaced out.
Role: The mediator. Keeps the conversation from becoming a monologue. The audience's entry point.
Voice: UnusedUmbrella

The Long Game

Twenty episodes of a producible, dialogue-driven short-form series builds three things simultaneously: an audience, a body of work, and a proof of concept. This is not the final destination. It is how you earn the right to make the bigger show.

The format is tight by design. If the show finds traction, the Adult Swim conversation becomes real. If it builds a following large enough, the next project has an audience already waiting before it releases.
